Chapter 1: A Short History of the Debate on Vatican II

The Second Vatican Council (1962-1965) did not end when the bishops went home; in many ways, it had just begun. The "debate on Vatican II" refers to the ongoing, often contentious, effort to interpret, apply, and define the legacy of the Council. This history can be understood as a series of distinct phases, each with its own key actors, events, and controversies.

Phase 1: Acknowledged, Received, Refused (1965–1980) - The Era of Implementation and Polarisation

This initial period was marked by a surge of energy and enthusiasm, but also by immediate and fierce opposition. It was a time of great change and growing division.

The Mainstream Reception

The vast majority of the Church embraced the Council's reforms. Theologians who had been the architects of the Council, like Karl Rahner, Yves Congar, and Edward Schillebeeckx, published influential commentaries explaining the documents. They saw Vatican II as the "beginning of a beginning," a starting point for a profound renewal of the Church.

Example: The Liturgical Reform. The most visible change was the reform of the Mass. Following the Council's constitution Sacrosanctum Concilium, new rites were developed, and the Mass began to be celebrated in vernacular languages (English, French, Spanish, etc.) instead of Latin. This was a direct application of the Council's call for "full, conscious, and active participation."

The Birth of Theological "Parties"

The unity of the Council's "progressive" majority quickly fractured.

Example: Concilium vs. Communio. In 1965, theologians like Rahner and Schillebeeckx founded the journal Concilium to push forward the "spirit" of Vatican II, emphasizing reform and engagement with the modern world. In response, a more cautious group of theologians, including Henri de Lubac, Hans Urs von Balthasar, and Joseph Ratzinger, founded the journal Communio in 1972. They feared that Concilium was going too far, too fast, and they sought to interpret the Council in stricter continuity with the Church's great tradition. This marked the beginning of the major theological "fault line" that would define the post-conciliar era.

The Radical Rejection

On the other end of the spectrum, a small but vocal minority rejected the Council outright.

Example: Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre. A French archbishop who had been a leader of the conservative minority at the Council, Lefebvre came to believe that Vatican II was a catastrophic break with Catholic Tradition. He particularly condemned its teachings on religious freedom, ecumenism, and the new Mass. In 1970, he founded the Society of St. Pius X (SSPX) to preserve the pre-Vatican II liturgy and theology, a move that would eventually lead to his excommunication and a formal schism.

Phase 2: Celebrated and Enforced (1980–1990) - The Wojtyła Pontificate

The election of Pope John Paul II in 1978, a Council Father himself, inaugurated a new phase. His goal was to be the primary interpreter and enforcer of the Council, steering a course between what he saw as the extremes of radical progressivism and traditionalist rejection.

The Pope as Chief Interpreter

John Paul II saw it as his mission to implement the "authentic" Vatican II. He traveled the world, bringing the Council's message to a global audience. He wrote encyclicals that developed conciliar themes, such as ecumenism in Ut Unum Sint.

The "Restoration" and Centralisation

While celebrating the Council, the pontificate was also marked by a "restoration" of order and a re-centralization of authority in Rome. The Vatican grew wary of what it saw as abuses and misinterpretations of the Council's teachings.

Example: The 1985 Extraordinary Synod of Bishops. John Paul II convened this synod to assess the reception of Vatican II twenty years on. The final report from the synod, heavily influenced by Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, emphasized interpreting the Council through a "hermeneutic of continuity." It downplayed the "People of God" ecclesiology in favor of a "theology of communion" and called for the creation of a universal catechism, which became the Catechism of the Catholic Church (1992). This was seen by many as a move to rein in the more decentralizing and creative energies that the Council had unleashed.

Phase 3: Historicized (1990–2000) - The Battle of the Historians

This period saw the debate move into the academic arena. As the generation that experienced the Council began to pass away, a new effort emerged to understand Vatican II not just as a set of documents, but as a historical event.

The Rise of Historical Scholarship

The key development was the publication of the five-volume History of Vatican II, edited by Giuseppe Alberigo and a team of international scholars known as the "Bologna School."

Example: The "Bologna School" Interpretation. This project, based on unprecedented access to diaries, letters, and archives, argued that the "spirit" and "event" of the Council were crucial for understanding its documents. They portrayed Vatican II as a turning point, a positive rupture with the defensive, "fortress" mentality of the 19th-century Church.

The Counter-Narrative

This historical interpretation was immediately challenged by a more conservative group of historians and theologians, often based in Rome.

Example: The "Roman" Critique. Figures like Agostino Marchetto accused the Bologna School of being ideologically biased and of creating a "Vatican II of the historians" that was separate from the "Vatican II of the popes and the documents." This counter-narrative argued for a strict focus on the final texts, minimizing the significance of the historical debates and the "spirit" of the event.

Phase 4: A New Fight over Vatican II? (2005–Present) - The Ratzinger Pontificate and Beyond

The election of Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger as Pope Benedict XVI in 2005 brought the "battle for meaning" to the very highest level of the Church. As a leading peritus at the Council and a key figure in the post-conciliar debates, his pontificate was defined by his effort to establish an authoritative interpretation of Vatican II.

The Hermeneutic of Reform in Continuity

Benedict XVI's most famous contribution to the debate came in his December 22, 2005, address to the Roman Curia. In it, he explicitly rejected a "hermeneutic of discontinuity and rupture" and championed a "hermeneutic of reform" in continuity with the one tradition of the Church. This was the definitive papal endorsement of the Communio school's interpretation.

Liturgical "Reform of the Reform"

Benedict XVI took concrete steps to enact his interpretation.

Example: Summorum Pontificum. In 2007, he issued this document which liberalized the use of the pre-Vatican II Tridentine Mass, calling it the "Extraordinary Form" of the Roman Rite. This was seen by supporters as a generous gesture to heal the rift with traditionalists and recover a sense of reverence. Critics, however, saw it as a step backward from the Council's liturgical vision and a move that created two parallel liturgies within the Church.

This history shows that the debate over Vatican II is not merely an academic squabble. It is a living, breathing struggle over the identity and future direction of the Catholic Church, a "battle for meaning" that continues to this day.

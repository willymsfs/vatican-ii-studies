Chapter 2: Questioning the Legitimacy of Vatican II

While the vast majority of the Catholic Church accepted the Second Vatican Council as a valid and authoritative ecumenical council, a significant and persistent "questioning" of its legitimacy emerged almost immediately. This questioning went far beyond disagreements over how to interpret the Council's documents; it struck at the very root of the Council's authority, arguing that Vatican II represented a fundamental and illegitimate break with two millennia of Catholic Tradition. This challenge is primarily embodied by the traditionalist movement.

The Core of the Rejection: Vatican II as a "Rupture"

The central claim of those who question Vatican II's legitimacy is that it was not a reform in continuity with the past, but a revolutionary rupture. They see the Council not as an "updating" (aggiornamento) but as a surrender to the very forces of modernity that previous popes, like Pius IX in the Syllabus of Errors, had explicitly condemned.

Example: The "French Revolution in the Church." This is a famous phrase used by Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre to describe the Council. He argued that the revolutionary ideals of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" were smuggled into the Church under the guise of religious liberty, collegiality, and ecumenism, thereby corrupting its divine constitution.

Key Figures and Movements

Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre and the Society of St. Pius X (SSPX)

Who he was: A French archbishop who was a prominent member of the conservative minority (the Coetus Internationalis Patrum) during the Council. He was deeply respected for his missionary work in Africa.

His Argument: Lefebvre believed that Vatican II taught errors, particularly in its Declaration on Religious Liberty (Dignitatis Humanae). He argued that the traditional teaching—that the state has a duty to recognize the Catholic Church as the one true religion and suppress public error—was abandoned in favor of a liberal, Masonic ideal of freedom of conscience. He saw the new Mass (Novus Ordo Missae) as a "Protestantized" rite designed to please non-Catholics, which diminished the sacrificial nature of the Eucharist.

Concrete Action: In 1970, he founded the Society of St. Pius X (SSPX) as a priestly fraternity dedicated to preserving the Tridentine Latin Mass and pre-conciliar theology. This act of institutionalized dissent culminated in 1988 when he consecrated four bishops without papal approval, an act which resulted in his automatic excommunication and a formal schism.

Romano Amerio and the Intellectual Critique

Who he was: An Italian-Swiss theologian and classical philologist who served as an expert (peritus) to a bishop during the Council's preparatory phase. He was a highly respected intellectual who remained within the Church but became one of its sharpest internal critics.

His Argument: In his monumental 1985 book, Iota Unum: A Study of Change in the Catholic Church in the Twentieth Century, Amerio meticulously documented what he saw as a catastrophic "change" in the Church's substance after Vatican II. He argued that the Council's ambiguous language deliberately opened the door to a "circulation of errors" on everything from the nature of the priesthood and the liturgy to morality and the Church's relationship with the world. He saw Vatican II as the culmination of the Modernist heresy condemned by Pope St. Pius X in 1907.

Key Difference from Lefebvre: Unlike Lefebvre, Amerio never formally broke with the Church. His critique was that of a scholar arguing that the post-conciliar Church had lost its "doctrinal unity" and abandoned its own principles.

The Sedevacantists: The Most Extreme Position

Their Argument: This is the most radical fringe of the traditionalist movement. The term sedevacantist comes from the Latin sede vacante, meaning "the chair [of Peter] is vacant." They believe that the popes who presided over and implemented Vatican II (John XXIII, Paul VI, and their successors) were manifest heretics. By embracing the "heresies" of the Council, they argue, these men automatically lost the papal office. Therefore, the Holy See is vacant, and these men are not true popes but anti-popes.

Impact: This position places its adherents in a state of complete rupture, as they reject the entire hierarchy of the Church that is in communion with the post-conciliar popes.

The Main "Errors" Condemned by Traditionalists

Those who question the Council's legitimacy consistently point to three key areas as proof of its rupture with Tradition:

Religious Liberty

As mentioned, they see Dignitatis Humanae as a direct contradiction of the perennial teaching on the "Social Kingship of Christ," which demands that states recognize Christ and his Church. They view religious freedom as tantamount to religious indifferentism (the idea that all religions are equally valid).

Ecumenism

They reject the Council's friendly outreach to Protestant and Orthodox communities. They see the Decree on Ecumenism (Unitatis Redintegratio) as a betrayal of the dogma that "outside the Church there is no salvation" (Extra Ecclesiam nulla salus). For them, true unity can only be achieved by the "return" of heretics and schismatics to the one true Church, not through mutual dialogue and respect.

Collegiality

They view the doctrine of episcopal collegiality as an attack on the monarchical nature of the papacy defined at Vatican I. They see it as a "democratic" principle that undermines the Pope's supreme and immediate authority over the entire Church.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the questioning of Vatican II's legitimacy is not a simple disagreement but a profound rejection of the Council's authority. Led by figures like Archbishop Lefebvre and intellectually articulated by thinkers like Romano Amerio, this movement views the Council as a revolutionary event that broke with authentic Catholic Tradition, introducing fundamental errors into the life and teaching of the Church.

Chapter 4: The Church and the World: Augustinians and Thomists

One of the most profound intellectual dramas of the Second Vatican Council was the clash between two deep-seated theological traditions concerning the fundamental relationship between the Church and the modern world. This was not a simple political dispute between "liberals" and "conservatives," but a philosophical and theological fault line between two major schools of thought: the Neo-Augustinian and the "Progressive" Neo-Thomist. Their differing views on grace, nature, history, and culture created the intellectual backdrop for the Council's most significant debates, especially those surrounding the pastoral constitution Gaudium et Spes (The Church in the Modern World).

1. The Neo-Augustinian Tendency: Caution, Skepticism, and the "City of God"

This school of thought, drawing inspiration from St. Augustine of Hippo, tended to view the secular world with a degree of skepticism and caution. It emphasized the radical necessity of God's grace in a world marked by sin.

Core Beliefs:

Church vs. World: This view often sees a sharp distinction, even a rivalry, between the "City of God" (the Church) and the "City of Man" (the secular world). The world is seen as a place of potential darkness and error from which the Church must distinguish itself.

Pessimism about "Nature": It is wary of an overly optimistic view of human nature and progress. It stresses that human reason and culture, without the light of divine grace and revelation, are prone to corruption and pride.

Emphasis on Ressourcement: This school championed a "return to the sources," especially the Church Fathers (like Augustine), as the primary way to renew the Church, rather than adapting to modern philosophical trends.

Key Figures:

Joseph Ratzinger (the future Pope Benedict XVI): His theology is deeply Augustinian. He was critical of what he saw as the "naïve optimism" of Gaudium et Spes and later argued that the post-conciliar Church had often surrendered to a secular, worldly mentality. His focus on the liturgy and the transcendent mystery of God is a hallmark of this perspective.

Henri de Lubac: A leading figure of the Nouvelle Théologie, de Lubac was also deeply rooted in the Church Fathers. After the Council, he grew increasingly critical of what he saw as a "terrible decay" in the Church, which he attributed to a false interpretation of the Council that sought to dissolve the Church into the world.

Hans Urs von Balthasar: Though not a Council peritus, his influential theology shared this Augustinian skepticism. He famously wrote of the need for the Church to "raze the bastions" before the Council, but afterward felt that the opening to the world had been one-sided, lacking a corresponding deepening of its own unique Christian identity.

2. The "Progressive" Neo-Thomist Tendency: Dialogue, Optimism, and the "Signs of the Times"

This school, inspired by the method of St. Thomas Aquinas, was more optimistic about the Church's ability to engage with and "baptize" the best of modern thought, just as Aquinas had done with the philosophy of Aristotle in the 13th century.

Core Beliefs:

Grace Perfects Nature: This is a classic Thomist principle. This view holds that the secular world ("nature"), while fallen, is not totally corrupt. It possesses its own integrity and autonomy. Divine grace does not destroy nature but heals, elevates, and perfects it. Therefore, the Church can and should engage with the good found in modern science, philosophy, and culture.

Reading the "Signs of the Times": This school championed the idea that God's presence and action can be discerned in historical events and cultural movements. The Church has a duty to read these "signs of the times" to understand how to present the Gospel in a relevant way.

Emphasis on History and Incarnation: This view sees theology as needing to be historically conscious and incarnational, deeply embedded in the concrete reality of the world.

Key Figures:

Yves Congar: A Dominican theologian and one of the Council's most important architects. He argued for a historically aware theology and was a key proponent of the role of the laity and the importance of reading the "signs of the times."

Marie-Dominique Chenu: Congar's mentor, Chenu was a medieval historian who argued that the Gospel must be "incarnated" in every age. He saw the end of the "Constantinian era" (the alliance of throne and altar) as a positive development that freed the Church to engage the world in a new, more authentic way.

Karl Rahner: A German Jesuit whose "transcendental Thomism" sought to build a bridge between traditional Catholic thought and modern philosophy (especially Kant and Heidegger). His vision of the "anonymous Christian" reflects a deep optimism about God's grace at work throughout the world, even outside the visible Church.

The Clash in Practice: The Debate over Gaudium et Spes

This theological divide was not abstract; it played out directly on the floor of the Council.

Example: During the drafting of Gaudium et Spes, the Augustinians (like Ratzinger) criticized the text for its overly optimistic tone, its lack of emphasis on sin, and its potential to reduce the Gospel to a mere program for social improvement. The Thomists (like Chenu and Congar) defended the text's positive engagement with the world as a necessary pastoral and theological step, arguing that the Church could no longer afford to speak only to itself.

The final document is a synthesis of these two views, but its very existence and its overall tone of dialogue represent a significant victory for the Thomistic tendency. The ongoing "battle for meaning" in the post-conciliar Church is, in many ways, a continuation of this fundamental debate between the Augustinian and Thomist visions of the Church and its place in the world.

Chapter 5: The Clash of Narratives

In the decades following the Second Vatican Council, particularly after the 1985 Extraordinary Synod of Bishops, the debate over its meaning evolved into a "clash of narratives." This was no longer just a disagreement over the interpretation of specific texts, but a fundamental conflict between two competing stories about what Vatican II was and what it means for the Catholic Church. This clash is primarily fought between a narrative that sees the Council as a historical "event" of rupture and renewal, and one that sees it as a purely legislative assembly whose meaning is confined to its final, written "documents."

Narrative 1: Vatican II as a Historical "Event" (The Bologna School)

This narrative, most famously associated with a group of international historians led by Giuseppe Alberigo of Bologna, argues that to understand Vatican II, one must look beyond the final sixteen documents. The Council, in this view, was a dynamic historical event—a process, a turning point, a "spirit"—that is greater than the sum of its textual parts.

Core Beliefs:

The "Spirit" of the Council: This narrative emphasizes the importance of the "spirit of Vatican II," which they see as a decisive move away from the defensive, anti-modern "fortress" mentality of the 19th-century Church.

Rupture as Renewal: It views the Council as a necessary and positive rupture with the immediate past (the era of Popes Pius IX and Pius XII) in order to recover a more ancient and authentic tradition (the Bible and the Church Fathers).

History as Key: To understand the Council, one must study its history: the debates, the rejected drafts, the diaries of the participants, and the struggle between the conservative minority and the progressive majority. The meaning is found in the story of the Council.

Key Project and Figures:

The History of Vatican II (5 volumes): This massive, multi-authored project, edited by Giuseppe Alberigo and with key contributions from figures like Joseph Komonchak and Étienne Fouilloux, is the landmark achievement of this narrative. It meticulously documents the historical unfolding of the Council to support its interpretation.

John W. O'Malley: In his influential book, What Happened at Vatican II, Jesuit historian John O'Malley popularized this narrative for a wider audience. He focuses on the "language event" of the Council, arguing that its shift from a legislative, condemnatory style to a pastoral, invitational style was itself a revolutionary change.

Narrative 2: Vatican II as Legislative "Documents" (The Roman/Conservative Critique)

This narrative, which gained prominence in Rome and among more conservative theologians and historians, argues that the "event" and "spirit" are subjective and misleading categories. The true meaning of the Council, they contend, is to be found exclusively in the final, authoritative documents promulgated by the Pope.

Core Beliefs:

The "Letter" of the Council: This narrative insists on a strict adherence to the "letter" of the texts. Any interpretation that goes "beyond the documents" by appealing to a vague "spirit" is seen as an ideological hijacking of the Council's true intent.

Continuity as Essential: It vehemently rejects the language of "rupture." This narrative argues that Vatican II must be interpreted in strict continuity with the entire Catholic tradition, especially the Councils of Trent and Vatican I. It seeks to minimize any sense of novelty or change.

History as Suspect: This view is suspicious of the historical approach of the Bologna School, seeing it as a "deconstruction" of the Council's teaching that overemphasizes conflict and ignores the final consensus embodied in the approved texts.

Key Figures and Proponents:

Pope Benedict XVI: As Cardinal Ratzinger and later as Pope, he was the most powerful proponent of this narrative. His December 22, 2005 speech is the classic articulation of this view, explicitly contrasting a false "hermeneutic of discontinuity and rupture" (which he associated with the "event" narrative) with a true "hermeneutic of reform in continuity."

Agostino Marchetto: A Vatican diplomat and historian, Marchetto became the leading scholarly critic of the Bologna School. He argued that Alberigo and his colleagues had imposed a biased, "ultra-progressive" interpretation on the Council, creating a "false council" that did not reflect the mind of the popes and the majority of bishops.

The Clash in Practice: Ecclesiology and Liturgy

This clash of narratives is not abstract; it has concrete consequences for the life of the Church.

Example: The Liturgy

The "Event" Narrative sees the liturgical reform as the beginning of a process of renewal, empowering local cultures to continue adapting the liturgy (inculturation).

The "Documents" Narrative argues for a more "faithful" translation of the Latin texts and a "reform of the reform" that corrects what it sees as the abuses and excesses that came from appealing to the "spirit" of the Council rather than the letter of Sacrosanctum Concilium. Benedict XVI's liberalization of the pre-conciliar Mass in Summorum Pontificum is a direct result of this narrative.

Example: The Interpretation of subsistit in

The "Event" Narrative sees the change in Lumen Gentium from saying the Church of Christ is the Catholic Church to saying it subsists in the Catholic Church as a major historical and theological breakthrough, opening the door to a new ecumenical reality.

The "Documents" Narrative, as articulated by the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (under Cardinal Ratzinger), has insisted that this change "does not change the doctrine on the Church" but simply clarifies it, thereby minimizing its novelty and emphasizing continuity with pre-conciliar teaching.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the "Clash of Narratives" is the central intellectual struggle in the contemporary Church. It is a battle between two fundamentally different ways of remembering and understanding Vatican II, with profound implications for theology, liturgy, and the future direction of Catholicism.

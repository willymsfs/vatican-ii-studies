Chapter 6: Macro-Issues of the Debate About Vatican II

After examining the history, the competing narratives, and the theological schools of thought, Faggioli identifies several "macro-issues" that lie at the heart of the ongoing debate about Vatican II. These are not merely academic disputes but fundamental questions about the nature of the Catholic Church, its relationship to history, and its mission in the world. These macro-issues help explain why the "battle for meaning" continues to be so intense and consequential.

1. Was Vatican II an End or a Beginning?

This is perhaps the most fundamental question in the entire debate.

Vatican II as an "End"

This view sees the Council as the completion of a process of renewal that began in the 19th century. The Church has now "updated" itself (aggiornamento) and found its proper relationship with the modern world. The task now is to implement the Council's teachings faithfully, without further innovation.

Example: Pope Benedict XVI's approach can be seen as representing this view. His emphasis on the "hermeneutic of reform in continuity" suggests that the Council has provided the definitive framework for understanding the Church's relationship to modernity. The task is not to go beyond the Council but to implement it correctly.

Vatican II as a "Beginning"

This view, famously articulated by Karl Rahner, sees the Council as the "beginning of a beginning." The Church has only started the process of becoming a truly global, multicultural "World Church." Much more change and adaptation is needed.

Example: Liberation theologians in Latin America and inculturation theologians in Africa and Asia often take this view. They see Vatican II as having opened the door to a more radical transformation of the Church that is still underway.

2. The Question of Continuity vs. Discontinuity

This macro-issue concerns how Vatican II relates to the broader tradition of the Catholic Church.

The Continuity Position

This view insists that Vatican II must be interpreted in strict continuity with the entire Catholic tradition. Any apparent novelties are simply new ways of expressing perennial truths.

Example: The interpretation of the phrase "subsistit in" in Lumen Gentium. The Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, under Cardinal Ratzinger, argued that this phrase does not represent a change in the Church's understanding of itself but simply a clarification of existing doctrine.

The Discontinuity Position

This view argues that Vatican II represents a legitimate and necessary break with certain aspects of the Church's recent past in order to recover more ancient and authentic traditions.

Example: The Council's teaching on religious freedom in Dignitatis Humanae is seen by this view as a legitimate reversal of 19th-century papal teaching that had condemned religious liberty. This reversal is justified as a return to the Gospel's respect for human dignity and freedom of conscience.

3. The Role of History in Theology

This macro-issue concerns whether and how historical consciousness should influence theological understanding.

The Historical Approach

This view, championed by theologians like Yves Congar and Marie-Dominique Chenu, argues that theology must be historically conscious. The Church's understanding of its own teaching develops over time, and this development must be taken seriously.

Example: The development of the Church's social teaching from Pope Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum to Vatican II's Gaudium et Spes is seen as a legitimate evolution in response to changing historical circumstances.

The Ahistorical Approach

This view is more suspicious of historical consciousness, fearing that it can lead to relativism. It prefers to emphasize the timeless, unchanging aspects of Catholic doctrine.

Example: The emphasis on the "deposit of faith" as a fixed body of truth that the Church guards and transmits without substantial change.

4. The Church's Relationship to Modernity

This macro-issue concerns how the Church should relate to the modern world and its values.

The Dialogue Position

This view, reflected in Gaudium et Spes, sees the Church as called to engage in dialogue with the modern world, learning from its insights while also offering its own wisdom.

Example: The Church's engagement with human rights discourse, seeing it as compatible with and even rooted in Christian anthropology.

The Prophetic Position

This view sees the Church as primarily called to be a prophetic voice against the errors and excesses of modernity, maintaining a critical distance from secular culture.

Example: The Church's opposition to certain aspects of sexual liberation and bioethical developments, seen as defending human dignity against reductive materialism.

5. The Nature of Papal Authority

This macro-issue concerns how Vatican II's teaching on collegiality relates to Vatican I's definition of papal primacy.

The Collegial Emphasis

This view emphasizes the collegial dimension of Church governance, seeing bishops as true partners with the Pope in governing the universal Church.

Example: The call for more meaningful episcopal synods and greater consultation with local churches in papal decision-making.

The Papal Emphasis

This view emphasizes the Pope's supreme authority and sees collegiality as a consultative rather than a deliberative principle.

Example: The centralization of authority in Rome during the pontificates of John Paul II and Benedict XVI, with limited real authority given to episcopal conferences.

6. The Question of Religious Pluralism

This macro-issue concerns how the Church should understand and relate to other religions.

The Inclusivist Position

This view, reflected in Nostra Aetate, sees other religions as containing elements of truth and grace, while maintaining that the fullness of revelation is found in Christ and the Catholic Church.

Example: Interreligious dialogue and cooperation, while maintaining the Church's missionary mandate.

The Exclusivist Position

This view is more skeptical of other religions, emphasizing the unique and exclusive role of Christ and the Catholic Church in salvation.

Example: The controversy over the document Dominus Iesus (2000), which reasserted the Church's claims about its unique role in salvation.

Conclusion

These macro-issues demonstrate that the debate over Vatican II is not merely about historical interpretation but about fundamental questions of Catholic identity, authority, and mission. The "battle for meaning" continues because these questions touch on the very heart of what it means to be Catholic in the modern world.
